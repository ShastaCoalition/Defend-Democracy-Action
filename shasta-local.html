📅 Public Meetings
Board meetings are held Tuesdays at 9:00 a.m. in Board Chambers, 1450 Court Street, Suite 263, Redding. Agendas are posted in advance at shastacounty.gov. Meetings are open to the public and include a public comment period.

How to make public comment at a Board meeting:

  1. Check the posted agenda online before the meeting to find the items relevant to you.
  2. Arrive early and sign up to speak — sign-up sheets are at the clerk's desk or the entrance.
  3. You typically have 3 minutes. State your name, your district, and your position clearly at the start.
  4. Be specific: reference the agenda item number and ask for a concrete action.
  5. You may also submit written comments to the Clerk of the Board in advance: [email protected]

Local Issues to WatchWhat's at Stake in Shasta County Right Now

Two issues are drawing significant community attention heading into the 2026 election cycle. Both are being covered by local independent outlets Shasta Scout, A News Cafe, and the Redding Record Searchlight.

JUNE 2026 PRIMARY District 1 Board of Supervisors: Crye vs. Resner

Erin Resner — former Redding City Council member and mayor, Dutch Bros franchise co-owner, and Shasta County native — is running again for the District 1 seat currently held by Kevin Crye. In 2022, Crye defeated Resner by just 90 votes out of nearly 11,000 cast. Resner has since served a full term on the Redding City Council and is running on a platform of fiscal accountability, transparency, and restoring civil governance to the board.

Key issues in the race:

  • Developer impact fees: Crye led the elimination of developer impact fees, claiming it would spur housing construction and lower home prices. Neither happened — housing starts did not meaningfully increase, and the county lost a significant revenue stream with nothing to show for it.
  • Supervisors' raises vs. IHSS workers: The board voted itself a 4% raise while simultaneously denying any raise to In-Home Supportive Services (IHSS) workers — among the county's lowest-paid employees — claiming there was no money available. Critics note that reinstating the impact fees alone could fund worker pay increases.
  • True North Behavioral Health grant: A proposed behavioral health campus that would have brought $150 million in state funding and $50 million in private investment to Shasta County was denied state grant funding. Resner has publicly attributed the loss to Crye's vocal opposition to the project.
  • Board conduct: Crye survived a recall attempt in 2023 by just 50 votes. He has been one of the most prominent figures in the faction that critics describe as having turned county board meetings into a focal point of far-right disruption. He is backed by the Liberty Committee.

📌 Resner is endorsed by ShastaVote PAC. The June 2026 primary will determine whether this race goes to a November runoff.

ELECTION INTEGRITY WATCH Registrar of Voters: The Curtis Controversy

The Shasta County Registrar of Voters office has been the center of significant controversy since Clint Curtis was appointed by a 3-2 board vote on April 30, 2025 — with Supervisors Crye, Harmon, and Kelstrom voting in favor, and Supervisors Long and Plummer dissenting in favor of the far more experienced local candidate.

Background:

  • The vacancy: The previous ROV, Cathy Darling Allen, had widespread community support across party lines. She resigned due to health problems that her supporters attribute directly to the sustained harassment and hostile environment created by election-denial activists — including members of the board majority. Her successor, interim ROV Tom Toller, also resigned citing health reasons.
  • Who was passed over: Joanna Francescut had served 17 years in the elections office, rising to Assistant ROV under both Darling Allen and Toller. She ran the March 2024 primary on her own while Darling Allen was on medical leave. Both dissenting supervisors and Toller himself endorsed her as his successor. She was among five finalists but was passed over by the board majority in favor of Curtis.
  • Curtis's first act: On his fifth day on the job, Curtis fired Francescut — by written notice, without meeting with her, without giving her the opportunity to retrieve her belongings from her office. "I am deeply disappointed that since his appointment, the new county clerk did not make any effort to meet with me or provide an opportunity to work collaboratively," Francescut said in a written statement. Curtis later publicly called for Francescut — his campaign opponent — to be federally indicted, based on allegations that have not been substantiated.
  • His hire: Curtis appointed Brent Turner of San Francisco as assistant ROV — without posting a job listing or running a competitive hiring process. Turner has no prior government experience. Curtis has known Turner for 25 years. This is the first government role either man has held in Shasta County.
  • Curtis's background: Curtis is an election lawyer with offices in New York and Florida. He falsely claimed on his ROV job application that he had never been discharged or forced to resign — when asked about it, he said his contract was "ended," not terminated, a distinction critics call semantic. When asked directly by A News Café, he blamed former Florida Gov. Jeb Bush for his 2002 firing from the Florida Dept. of Transportation. He is endorsed by MyPillow founder and election conspiracy theorist Mike Lindell.
  • Election denier staff: Curtis has hired known local election deniers — community members who spent years harassing elections staff and challenging election results without evidence — to work in the elections office alongside the professional staff they previously harangued. The SF Chronicle reported that assistant ROV Brent Turner described this as "a bit of a social experiment." Public records and community reporting identify figures including Laura Hobbs, a vocal election denier and initiative organizer, among those associated with the office. Critics allege at least one hire has a troubled prior work history, including an alleged incident involving brandishing a firearm at a previous job — though this has not been independently confirmed in published reporting available to date. Community members have raised concerns about unqualified promotions that may expose the county to discrimination lawsuits.
  • Lied to the Secretary of State: Curtis publicly claimed that California Secretary of State Shirley Weber had approved the changes he was making to Shasta County's election system. The Secretary of State's office confirmed that Curtis had never contacted them. The board took no action.
  • Viral after-hours video: Surveillance footage released through a Public Records Act request showed Curtis — shirtless — and his non-employee wife moving a county elections office safe on a weekend using a car jack, a two-by-four, and moving straps after hours. The video went viral with over 20,000 views and raised questions about security protocols, liability, and unauthorized use of county facilities.

⚠ The Riverside Electioneering Scandal — and the Oath Keeper Running for Governor:

On January 15, 2026, Curtis hosted a tour of the Shasta County elections office for six candidates running for Governor of California, along with noted election conspiracy theorist Doug Frank. During the tour, Curtis claimed that the previous administration — which includes Joanna Francescut, his opponent in the June 2026 election — had engaged in criminal ballot stuffing. Supervisor Allen Long called it potential electioneering on county property and county time — a violation of both state law and county policy. The Board of Supervisors discussed it at two consecutive meetings and then dropped the inquiry entirely.

One of the governor candidates at that tour was Riverside County Sheriff Chad Bianco — a former member of the Oath Keepers, the far-right militia implicated in the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack. Bianco's membership was exposed in a 2021 data breach; he acknowledged paying for a one-year membership in 2014. He is currently one of the top Republican contenders in the 2026 California governor's race.

Shortly after that tour, Curtis traveled to Riverside County and presented to their Board of Supervisors — publicly promoting his election methods while a local citizen group, the Riverside Election Integrity Team (REIT), was claiming roughly 45,000 excess ballots in Riverside's November 2025 election. State and county officials confirmed the actual discrepancy was approximately 103 ballots — a rounding difference in handwritten intake logs kept by temporary workers after a very long election night.

In late February 2026, Sheriff Bianco launched a formal investigation and seized over 650,000 ballots from the Riverside elections office. California Attorney General Rob Bonta called the seizure "unacceptable" and "unprecedented," stating it was designed to sow distrust in elections. The state Secretary of State's office said the investigation was "not normal behavior for any sheriff."

Shasta's own Curtis revealed the Riverside investigation in an offhand remark at a February 24 Shasta County Board of Supervisors meeting — while defending himself against his own misconduct allegations — before the Riverside Sheriff's Office had made any public announcement. The LA Times, Washington Post, and SF Chronicle all covered the Bianco ballot seizure and the network of election conspiracy coordination. The Chronicle described Curtis as "a polarizing elections skeptic who sometimes skews too controversial for the far-right Shasta County supervisors who hired him."

What happens next: Francescut is running against Curtis for the permanent ROV position in the June 2026 primary. A community group called "Shasta Exposed" is circulating a petition calling for Curtis and Turner to be placed on paid leave pending an investigation into election mismanagement. Curtis has threatened a defamation lawsuit in response. This race is a direct referendum on the legitimacy and competence of the county's election administration — and on who controls how Shasta County's votes are counted in November 2026 and beyond.

Why This Office Matters for Civil Rights The Sheriff is an elected position — directly accountable to voters, not to the county administrator or the Board of Supervisors. The Sheriff has broad discretionary authority over how laws are enforced in unincorporated Shasta County, including decisions on cooperation with federal agencies, use-of-force policies, and jail conditions. Residents have the right to contact this office with policy concerns, and hold it accountable at the ballot box.
  • To raise a concern about a deputy's conduct, contact the Sheriff's Office and request to speak with a supervisor or Internal Affairs.
