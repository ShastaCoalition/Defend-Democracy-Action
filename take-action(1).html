Make Phone Calls

Anti-Corruption: End Corruption Now Legislative Package

Rep. Joe Neguse (CO-02) has organized a bipartisan group of House members around these seven bills. Read the full agenda on Rep. Neguse's official site →

  • Close the Revolving Door Act — Lifetime ban on members of Congress becoming paid lobbyists after leaving office
  • Restoring Integrity in Democracy Resolution — Bans members from sitting on corporate boards while in office
  • TRUST in Congress Act — Bans members, their spouses, and dependent children from trading individual stocks
  • No Corporate Crooks Act — Bars CEOs convicted of financial crimes from serving in the executive branch
  • Closing Bribery Loopholes Act — Fixes bribery definitions weakened by recent court decisions
  • The Musk Act — Requires government employees to recuse themselves from matters affecting their own or their former employers' finances
  • Executive Oversight Protection — Protects nonpartisan watchdogs overseeing the executive branch
📊 This isn't partisan — it's popular. Research shows 86% of Republicans, 88% of Democrats, and 81% of independents support banning congressional stock trading. These bills will only pass if constituents demand it. Call your representatives and ask them to co-sponsor the End Corruption Now package.

Collective ActionGeneral Strikes & Economic Boycotts

A general strike is when workers across multiple industries simultaneously refuse to work, applying economic pressure to force political or social change. General strikes have been profoundly effective throughout history — they are among the most powerful tools citizens have ever used to oppose unjust governments and demand accountability. When other forms of pressure fail, they are the logical next step.

Unlike a targeted labor strike aimed at one employer, a general strike challenges the political system itself. When large numbers of people stop participating in the economy, those in power are forced to pay attention in ways they cannot ignore.

Historical examples — it works

Where & WhenWhat HappenedOutcome
🇺🇸 Philadelphia, USA, 1835 America's first citywide general strike — 20,000 workers across 40 trades walked out demanding a ten-hour workday Won. Workers secured the ten-hour workday and set the model for labor organizing across Boston and New York
🇺🇸 San Francisco, USA, 1934 150,000 workers shut down the city in solidarity with dockworkers demanding union recognition. The National Guard occupied the waterfront; hundreds were arrested. Won. After four days, workers won arbitration, union recognition, and the hiring hall system — shifting power on the docks for generations
🇿🇦 South Africa, 1950–61 ANC-led strikes as part of the sustained anti-apartheid campaign Built mass resistance that ultimately ended apartheid
🇵🇱 Poland, 1988 Solidarity-led strikes across industries Won. Forced the communist government to negotiate; led directly to free elections
🇮🇸 Iceland, 1975 "Women's Day Off" — 90% of women across the country walked out Won. Major equal rights legislation passed within years
🇪🇸 Spain, 2010 & 2012 General strikes against austerity brought millions into the streets Forced government policy changes and shaped election outcomes
🗓️ Friday, May 1, 2026 — International May Day Mass Action

Everyone Can Do Something. Nobody Has To Do Everything.

On Friday, May 1, 2026, people across the United States and around the world are being called to take part in a coordinated mass action. This is not just an American movement — workers and citizens in countries across the globe are joining together on the same day to send an unmistakable message. Here's how you can participate at whatever level you're able:

  • 1
    If you have a job: Use a sick day or whatever paid time off your workplace allows — following your normal call-in procedure exactly. Talk to coworkers in person if you can. Build a group outside work platforms. The more people who participate, the safer everyone is.
  • 2
    If you're a student: Stay home or organize a walkout with classmates. Talk to people beforehand — in person is best. Coordinate in advance so you're not alone.
  • 3
    If you can't miss work or school: Don't spend money that day. Join a rally or march. Recruit your neighbors and family. Make your participation visible.
  • 4
    If you can't boycott everything: Boycott what you can. Where you shop, what you stream, what you buy — targeted choices add up at massive scale. Do what you're able to do.

"Nobody can do everything. But everyone can do something. And right now, their country needs them to do it."

From the Ground UpBuild Local Power

Every major national movement has local roots. The most durable change comes from people organizing in their own communities.

  • Run for local office. School board, city council, water district, planning commission — winnable races with direct daily impact.
  • Join or start a local Indivisible chapter: indivisible.org/act/local
  • Attend public meetings. City councils, county supervisors, school boards — all have public comment periods. Showing up on the record matters.
  • Connect with your neighbors. Mutual aid networks build community resilience and political solidarity at the same time.
